风吹麦浪 / Wind in the Wheat Waves

A song that makes me think of the golden wheat fields in Mo Yan (common u gotta know him, the first Chinese Nobel Literature laureate, bigggg deal)’s province. My province Shandong. Primitive, agricultural in some parts, hilly in others, full of magical melancholy realism in the vast fields and in the midst of thick forests. A nature-loving truant, I’ve dreamed about bounding o’er the mountain like a roe so many times… Yet I can’t play Miss Caulfield in the act. Shandong is no New York City where you can hide your rebellious brow under a red baseball cap and get your name on the billboards by kickstarting a rock band, it’s where everybody knew everybody and they honestly hope you’d stay in school and become a decent hardworking fellow. They also kinda hope you won’t become “somebody” and grow your roots permanently in a big city—home is always the best, Toto.

The people on the streets of Shandong have big, dark, silent eyes, field-working eyes that softly look at the truant student reproachfully: what are you doing, child? Why haven’t you a purpose in life… on this land people work until their spines turn into bendy bows. All arrows are shot into the bleeding soil to grow into saplings—this is the densely populated land of non-conflict, of chimney smoke over the grape vine-covered village rooftops, of poor rural education and suffering aspirations, of the most heavily-rivalled college entering exam, of taciturn parents who never understood you but will empty their pockets to get you through school, of acres and acres of golden wheat waves, as vivid as that neighbour girl’s eye…who only left a few words in your dreams before a moving van took her to a big city with lurid lights, who left behind her the fields’ long orange shadows, stretching endlessly towards home…

As an afterthought…The song also reminds me of an all time classic . This is originally from a poem by William Butler Yeats (1865-1939), with music by the English composer and conductor Benjamin Britten (1913-1976). Yeats was a master of poetic spirit and folk culture—do you see how evenly his rhymes are wrapped around the refrain, and how they contribute to the folklorish, fairy-tale-like mood? They go A B1 A B1, B2 B2 C B2. D E D E, B2 B3 C B3. Like different leaves that grow from the branch, the B rhyme has variations in 1. -eet, 2. -y/ee and 3. -ears to avoid monotony. The prolongated -ee sound sounds like a long sigh of loss, and the fact that the song always return to it almost symbolises the unalterable sad ending of the story. But isn’t that what coming out of the shadow of unhappy loss is about, always “returning” to that same old secret garden where we buried our memories and tears, hoping to encounter someone’s ghost feeding pigeons on a park bench, sipping coffee in the corner of the shop, sprinting down the checkered street of childhood…

Sometimes our souls just wish to go home.

It’s funny that almost none of my friends know the poem nor the famous Mr. Britten, but once I start humming “Down-by-the-Sally-Gardens…” they all clicked with it like: “Ohhhhh I knooow that one!” So promise me, give it a try, alright? Might bring back some old times.

As for myself…I used to date a classical music guy who loved and insisted upon the Anna Lucia Richter operatic version (he thought she was the goddess muse in this bleak music-less wasteland of a world). We were both big fans of Yeats and seriously dreamed about going to Ireland and the Isle of Innisfree together… Alas, didn’t last long.

Lastly, I want to give the translated Wind in the Wheat Waves to my beloved grandma Laolao, who used to take me to the playing ground at sunset and watched over me, always smiling so quietly.
